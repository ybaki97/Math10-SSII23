Sanity Check: We‚Äôre asking how does distance, the hour of the day, and the number of passengers influence how many minutes a taxi ride will take. Predictions:

  • Distance: We expect that the greater the distance, the longer the taxi ride will take.

  • Hour: Remember, we‚Äôre looking at 5:00pm and after. As we move away from rush hour (5:00pm), we expect the taxi ride to take less time.

  • Passengers: Do we expect the number of passengers to have an influence on the duration of the ride? We don‚Äôt expect this to have too much influence on the duration of the taxi ride, but we‚Äôll find out.

Now, let‚Äôs get the coefficients‚Ä¶

#["distance","hour","passengers"]
reg.coef_

array([ 2.394538  , -0.59737863, -0.08703218])

Very important topic: being able to interpret what these coefficients are saying. Pay particular attention to the signs.

The coefficient for distance is positive; this makes sense, because as the number of miles we travel increases, we expect the duration of the ride to take longer.

The coefficient for the hour is negative; this is saying that as the hour increases (remember we are looking after 5:00pm), the duration of the taxi ride is shorter. The reason we restricted to after 5:00pm, is because we don‚Äôt expect the duration to decrease consistently throughout the day. There is traffic in the morning, when we also expect the duration of the taxi ride to be longer. If we included all hours of the day, we could expect this coefficient to be less accurate.

The last coefficient deals with the number of passengers. While it is negative, it is very close to 0; this tells us that the number of passengers doesn‚Äôt really say much about the duration of the taxi ride. Notice: the number of passengers in the care is pretty small, so this coefficient multiplied with the number of passengers is still a pretty small number. We‚Äôll see examples later today where coefficients might be small, but the column they correspond to has huge numbers, we expect more of an influence of the coefficients in this case.

Reminder of the model:

\[ \text{duration} \approx c_0 + c_1*\text{distance} + c_2*\text{hour} + c_3*\text{passengers} \]

You can kind of think of the coefficients like partial derivatives. For example, as we increase the distance by 1 mile, we can expect the time to go up by 2.4 minutes.

How does the linear regression model work?#

First, we‚Äôll have a short iPad section on some of the math behind the linear regression that scikit-learn uses. We won‚Äôt go through all of the details, but it will be enought to try testing some examples by hand.

Here, we generate sample data.

X, y = make_regression(n_samples=10, n_features=1, bias=13, noise=25, random_state=115)

data = {"input":X.reshape(-1),"target":y}
